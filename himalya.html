The Himalayas

The Roof of the World

About the Himalayas

The Himalayas, translating to "Abode of Snow" in Sanskrit, form a majestic mountain range in Asia separating the plains of the Indian subcontinent from the Tibetan Plateau. Spanning across five countries—India, Nepal, Bhutan, China, and Pakistan—this colossal range is home to nine of the ten highest peaks on Earth, including Mount Everest, the world's tallest mountain. Beyond their sheer geological scale, the Himalayas act as the source of major river systems like the Ganges, Indus, and Brahmaputra, sustaining billions of lives.

Why You Should Visit

  • Unparalleled Trekking: From the Everest Base Camp to the Annapurna Circuit, the trails here offer the ultimate adventure for hikers of all levels.
  • Breathtaking Biodiversity: The region spans diverse ecosystems, sheltering rare wildlife like the snow leopard, red panda, and Himalayan black bear.
  • Spiritual Awakening: Dotted with ancient monasteries, temples, and prayer flags, the peaceful environment is a global destination for meditation and yoga.
  • Vibrant Cultures: Experience the warm hospitality, unique cuisines, and rich traditions of the Sherpa, Tibetan, and various mountain communities.

Myths & Historical Connections

The Himalayas are steeped in profound history and mythology, serving as a spiritual epicenter for Hinduism, Buddhism, and Jainism.

The Abode of Lord Shiva

In Hindu mythology, Mount Kailash is revered as the eternal home of Lord Shiva and Goddess Parvati. It is considered the spiritual center of the universe, drawing thousands of pilgrims annually despite the grueling terrain.

The Legend of the Yeti

Deep within the snowy folklore of the region lies the legend of the Yeti, or the "Abominable Snowman." For centuries, local Sherpa folklore has spoken of a large, ape-like creature roaming the high-altitude, unmapped snowy regions.

The Ancient Silk Road

Historically, the treacherous passes of the Himalayas were crucial arteries of the Silk Road. Ancient merchants braved the snow-capped routes to trade tea, salt, silk, and spices between Tibet, India, and Central Asia.
